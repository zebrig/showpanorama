Brian "New York City Boy" V.
Предисловие к творчеству влада бурьяна




Born? Certainly the Poet entered this world in the usual manner, and enjoyed the subsequent years of being a child-puddle. Warm, heavy, sitting in the middle of the room looking at his fingers. Making nonsense noises. Bumping his head off the wall and table, biting his tongue with new teeth, swallowing water down the wrong pipe and coughing it up again---these were his primary activities. Sticking his finger in an electric socket, screaming, and then cooing.

Growing wiser, the Poet soon understood "suck" and "spit," along with other essential dichotomies such as "open/shut" and "right/left." His young tot mind was wiring itself like a needlepoint stitch, or those computer punch cards Papa worked on all day. The Poet understood hunger and satiety. He spen tlife until age 16 in this manner, his mind working like a light switch,engine cylinder, or toilet seat, all up or down. There seemed to be a great future in computer punch cards, and the Poet planned on entering the local polytechnic institute.

When did the Poet recognize his true human force, however? When did he stare at some polluted sky and say, "this is as serious as my life?" It happened one night in Moscow, walking home from a party. He passed a dead body lying in a gutter. He looked at it as he passed, and only fully turned and stopped a dozen strides later. The actual corpse was not very upsetting; what caused distress was the audible hum and hiss of the electric lines overhead. How long have they been making that sound? It was a hum as if all the spaces between all objects were fuzzy, crackling, and static-filled. At this moment, the Poet realized that every second contains an eternity, everything that exists and does not exist. Yet this man had only recently died... the Poet felt time pulsing behind his eyeballs. Time was pouring through like neutrinos, like particles they measure with lead tanks of heavy water, buried in a mountain. Time was a fragmented force moving at different speeds through static bodies. This dead man had experienced a great and sudden acceleration.

That sky looked like a half shell: shimmering and pearl-spotted from heat lightning and factory soot, a real man trap. Scanning the horizons, the Poet saw the shell come down and seal the inky-pulp lives of everyone. "Now we're on the inside," he thought. A fantasy and fact were born at this moment, and Death of the Poet is where they will meet.
Brian V.,
straight from the United States
